Why pre-cooling matters

The hidden cost of forced-air cooling alone

Cut flowers entering the cold chain with residual field heat or surface moisture face a documented set of risks — even when reefer containers run at 1°C and 80% RH. Operators relying solely on forced-air cooling rooms consistently lose 5–8% of shipped volume to these mechanisms.

Botrytis germination

Free water on petals at 1°C creates ideal germination conditions. Botrytis cinerea is the leading cause of arrival-quality claims and rejected pallets.

Microclimate hot spots

Inside cartons, local humidity can hit 100% and respiration accelerates — especially in pallet cores that take 12–24 hours to fully reach target temperature.

Freezing damage

Residual droplets contacting sub-zero evaporator surfaces cause localized freezing, water-soaking, translucent petals, and browning.

Grade downgrades

Cosmetic and vase-life issues drop A-grade stems to B-grade in transit, eroding FOB pricing — and the gap is widest on premium varieties.

Importer claims

Quality-related credits typically run 1.5–3% of FOB revenue. Premium importers apply tighter tolerances and faster claims processes.

Shortened vase life

Accelerated ethylene production means rejections at retail and credits back to the grower — costs that compound across the season.

How the numbers work

Methodology & assumptions

Every figure in the calculator traces back to a documented industry assumption or observed performance metric. Adjust any input to match your operation — the model is intentionally conservative.

Yield per hectare

1,000,000 stems/ha/yr is the standard reference for Colombian and Ecuadorian export rose operations. Carnations and chrysanthemums achieve higher density per hectare; hydrangea is substantially lower (100K–250K stems/ha) at much higher FOB.

FOB price per stem

$0.25 is a blended commodity-rose average. Premium roses run $0.40–$0.60, garden roses $0.80–$1.50, hydrangea $1.50–$3.00, carnations $0.08–$0.12. Model your variety mix accordingly.

Baseline waste (without VCC)

5%–8% reflects industry observation for operations relying on forced-air cooling rooms only. The 6.5% midpoint is the default; peak-season rates (Valentine's, Mother's Day) are typically higher as cold rooms over-load.

Post-VCC waste

1%–3% reflects observed performance in farms running vacuum cooling on full export volume. The 2% midpoint is the default. Operations with high Botrytis pressure or long transit chains typically see larger gains.

Importer claims

Quality-related credits commonly run 1.5%–3% of FOB revenue and typically drop to 0.5%–1% post-VCC. The default models a 1.5 percentage-point reduction — conservative for premium operations.

Grade retention

Forced-air-only operations see 10%–15% of stems downgraded in transit. Vacuum cooling preserves grade for ~80%+ of these stems. Model premium operations at 2.0% to reflect wider A→B price differentials.

Energy savings

Vacuum chambers consume more peak power for 20–30 minutes versus 12–24 hours of cold-room operation, reducing total kWh per kilogram of product cooled by 40%–60%.

System investment

$180K–$250K installed for commodity-class 50-ha operations; $200K–$280K for premium-class chambers with enhanced controls and gentler vacuum profiles. Final pricing depends on chamber size, controls, and site preparation.

About Mauricio Gleiser

Mauricio Gleiser is the Founder and Managing Partner of Vacuum Cooling Colombia SAS, a pioneer in vacuum cooling technology for Colombia's cut-flower export industry. A mechanical engineer trained at Universidad Nacional de Ingeniería in Lima, Peru, Mauricio has spent more than two decades introducing and refining vacuum cooling for fresh-cut flowers — beginning with the first commercial vacuum cooling operation at Bogotá's cargo terminal in 2001.

Today, Vacuum Cooling Colombia operates ICA-approved origin inspection facilities in Funza (Bogotá) and Rionegro (Medellín), serving florists, importers, producers, and large retailers across the United States, Europe, and Japan. Mauricio is an active member of PMA (Produce Marketing Association) and WFFSA (Wholesale Florist & Florist Supplier Association), and a founding member of Colombia's Certified Cold Chain Alliance.

He writes and speaks frequently on cold-chain integrity, sustainable maritime export of cut flowers, and the role of pre-shipment cooling in protecting product quality and reducing post-harvest waste.
